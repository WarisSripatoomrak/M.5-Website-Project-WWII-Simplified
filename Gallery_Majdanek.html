Remains at the Majdanek Camp (Lubin, Poland: October 1941 - July 1944)

KL Majdanek Mausoleum Remains at the Majdanek Camp.

    1. About 360,000 died:

      1. 60% died from starving, being tormented, and diseases.
      2. 40% died from mass firings and toxic gases (carbon monoxide and Zyklon B).

　　　The German concentration camp Majdanek was used for mass extermination. Aside from the hardship of living conditions, employment, and disease, the high death rate was exacerbated by direct methods of murder such as drowning in sewage pits, hangings, beatings, or phenol injections.

　　　The firing squads and gas chambers were the most lethal. Executions took place during the camp's operation. On November 3, 1943, about 18,000 Jews were killed at Majdanek and other Lublin camps in a single day. Similar killings of Polish inmates arriving from the Castle jail began in the fall of 1943 at KL Lublin. There have also been reports of SS members shooting individuals for fun.

　　　The victims' bodies were initially buried in mass graves before being burnt at crematoriums or on crematory pyres. After being mixed with soil and rubbish, the ashes will be used as fertiliser. Investigators uncovered 1,300 cubic metres of compost containing human bones following the camp's evacuation.
